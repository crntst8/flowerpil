# UX.md
## Laws of UX (Distilled)

A set of psychological principles commonly used in UX/UI design to improve usability, decision-making, and user satisfaction.

---

# Heuristics

## Aesthetic–Usability Effect
Users perceive aesthetically pleasing designs as easier to use.

**Takeaways**
- Visual polish increases perceived usability.
- Clean layouts reduce user resistance.
- Good aesthetics can improve tolerance for minor usability issues.

---

## Fitts’s Law
The time required to reach a target depends on its size and distance.

**Takeaways**
- Make important targets large.
- Place frequently used actions close to the user’s cursor or thumb.
- Avoid small clickable elements.
- Edge and corner targets are faster to acquire.

---

## Goal-Gradient Effect
Motivation increases as users approach goal completion.

**Takeaways**
- Show visible progress indicators.
- Break tasks into stages.
- Provide feedback when progress is made.
- Encourage completion with milestone markers.

---

## Hick’s Law
Decision time increases with the number and complexity of choices.

**Takeaways**
- Reduce the number of options presented at once.
- Use progressive disclosure.
- Group related options together.
- Provide defaults where possible.

---

## Jakob’s Law
Users prefer interfaces that work similarly to ones they already know.

**Takeaways**
- Follow established UI conventions.
- Avoid reinventing standard interaction patterns.
- Align with familiar navigation structures.
- Use recognizable UI components.

---

## Miller’s Law
The average person can hold about seven items in working memory.

**Takeaways**
- Chunk information into smaller groups.
- Avoid presenting long lists of unrelated items.
- Use visual grouping to support memory.
- Simplify multi-step instructions.

---

## Parkinson’s Law
Work expands to fill the time available for completion.

**Takeaways**
- Reduce unnecessary steps in workflows.
- Encourage faster completion with streamlined interfaces.
- Avoid adding unnecessary friction in processes.

---

# Gestalt Principles

## Law of Common Region
Elements inside the same visual boundary are perceived as related.

**Takeaways**
- Use containers, cards, or panels to group related items.
- Separate unrelated sections visually.

---

## Law of Proximity
Objects placed near each other are perceived as related.

**Takeaways**
- Group related UI elements closely.
- Increase spacing between unrelated elements.
- Use spacing as a primary organizational tool.

---

## Law of Prägnanz
People interpret complex visuals in the simplest possible form.

**Takeaways**
- Reduce visual complexity.
- Use clear shapes and hierarchy.
- Avoid unnecessary decorative elements.

---

## Law of Similarity
Elements that look similar are perceived as part of the same group.

**Takeaways**
- Use consistent colors, shapes, and typography for related items.
- Maintain consistent styling for interactive elements.
- Use visual differences to highlight important actions.

---

## Law of Uniform Connectedness
Elements that are visually connected are perceived as more related than those that are not.

**Takeaways**
- Use lines, borders, and connectors to show relationships.
- Avoid ambiguous relationships between elements.

---

# Cognitive Bias

## Peak–End Rule
Users judge an experience largely by its most intense moment and its ending.

**Takeaways**
- Design strong positive moments during key interactions.
- Ensure task completion feels satisfying.
- Minimize frustration at the end of workflows.

---

## Serial Position Effect
Items at the beginning and end of lists are remembered better than those in the middle.

**Takeaways**
- Place important items at the beginning or end of lists.
- Avoid hiding critical actions in the middle of menus.

---

## Von Restorff Effect
Items that stand out from their surroundings are more likely to be remembered.

**Takeaways**
- Use contrast to highlight primary actions.
- Limit the number of highlighted elements.
- Avoid making everything visually loud.

---

## Zeigarnik Effect
People remember unfinished tasks better than completed ones.

**Takeaways**
- Show incomplete tasks or progress indicators.
- Encourage task completion with reminders.
- Surface partially completed workflows.

---

# Additional Principles

## Doherty Threshold
Users remain engaged when system response times stay under roughly 400 ms.

**Takeaways**
- Optimize performance for responsiveness.
- Provide immediate feedback for user actions.
- Use loading indicators for longer operations.

---

## Occam’s Razor
The simplest solution that achieves the goal is usually best.

**Takeaways**
- Avoid unnecessary features.
- Simplify interface flows.
- Prioritize clarity over complexity.

---

## Pareto Principle
A small number of features usually produce the majority of value.

**Takeaways**
- Focus design effort on the most-used features.
- Identify the core user actions.
- Optimize the critical user paths.

---

## Postel’s Law
Systems should accept flexible input but produce consistent output.

**Takeaways**
- Accept variations in user input where possible.
- Provide clear and structured output.
- Design forgiving input validation.

---

## Tesler’s Law
Every system has inherent complexity that cannot be removed, only shifted.

**Takeaways**
- Move complexity away from the user where possible.
- Use automation to handle system complexity.
- Simplify user-facing interfaces even if internal systems become more complex.

---

# Supporting Concepts

## Flow
A state of deep focus that occurs when challenges match a user’s skill level.

**Takeaways**
- Avoid unnecessary interruptions.
- Maintain consistent interaction patterns.
- Provide clear feedback to keep users engaged.

---

## Selective Attention
Users focus only on elements relevant to their goals.

**Takeaways**
- Minimize visual clutter.
- Highlight key actions clearly.
- Remove unnecessary information.

---

## Working Memory
A limited cognitive system used for temporarily holding information.

**Takeaways**
- Avoid requiring users to remember information across steps.
- Show necessary information when needed.
- Reduce cognitive load through clear interface structure.
